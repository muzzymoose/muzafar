Overview

Employee turnover is one of the most critical challenges facing modern organizations, with the cost of replacing a single employee often exceeding 200% of their annual salary. This project leverages machine learning to predict which employees are at risk of leaving, enabling HR teams to intervene proactively and implement targeted retention strategies.

The Challenge

Organizations typically react to employee departures rather than preventing them. By the time an employee submits their resignation, it's often too late to retain them. The HR department lacked a systematic approach to identify at-risk employees and understand the factors driving turnover.

The challenge was to build a predictive model that could accurately forecast employee attrition while also providing interpretable insights into the key drivers of churn, enabling data-driven retention strategies.

Approach

Data Exploration & Understanding

Conducted comprehensive exploratory data analysis on employee records containing 35 features including demographics, job characteristics, performance ratings, compensation, and work-life balance indicators. Identified significant class imbalance with only 16.1% of employees having left the company.

Feature Engineering

Created new meaningful features to capture employee satisfaction signals:

  • Tenure indicators: Years since last promotion, time in current role
  • Compensation ratios: Salary relative to department average
  • Work-life metrics: Overtime frequency, travel requirements
  • Career progression: Promotion velocity, job level trajectory

Handling Class Imbalance

Implemented SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) to address the class imbalance problem, ensuring the model doesn't simply predict that all employees will stay, which would yield high accuracy but be useless for HR decision-making.

Model Development & Selection

Evaluated multiple classification algorithms including Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost, and Support Vector Machines. Selected Random Forest as the final model based on its superior performance in identifying at-risk employees (high recall for churn class) while maintaining overall accuracy.

Key Finding: The model achieved 87% accuracy with 78% recall for the attrition class, meaning it successfully identifies nearly 4 out of 5 employees who will leave, giving HR ample opportunity to intervene.

Top Predictive Factors

Feature importance analysis revealed the most significant drivers of employee attrition:

1. Overtime Work (Importance: 0.24)

Employees working frequent overtime were 3.2 times more likely to leave. This highlights the critical importance of work-life balance and sustainable workload management.

2. Years Since Last Promotion (Importance: 0.18)

Employees who hadn't been promoted in over 5 years showed significantly higher attrition rates. Career stagnation emerged as a critical retention risk.

3. Monthly Income (Importance: 0.16)

Compensation below market rates strongly predicted turnover, particularly for high performers. Salary competitiveness proved essential for retention.

4. Job Satisfaction (Importance: 0.14)

Low job satisfaction ratings (1-2 on a 4-point scale) were powerful predictors of departure within 6 months.

5. Distance From Home (Importance: 0.11)

Commute distances over 20 km correlated with higher attrition, especially among junior employees. Remote work options could mitigate this risk.

Other Significant Factors

  • Work-life balance ratings
  • Number of companies worked at previously
  • Years with current manager
  • Environmental satisfaction

Business Impact: By identifying 78% of potential departures before they occur, the model enables proactive interventions that could save the company an estimated $2.4M annually in replacement costs, assuming average replacement cost of $75K per employee.

Actionable Insights for HR

Immediate Interventions

  • Overtime Audit: Review and redistribute workload for employees consistently working overtime
  • Promotion Pipeline: Establish clear career progression timelines, especially for employees approaching 5 years without promotion
  • Compensation Review: Benchmark salaries against market rates, prioritizing high performers with below-average pay
  • Stay Interviews: Conduct proactive conversations with employees flagged as high-risk by the model

Strategic Initiatives

  • Implement flexible work arrangements to address commute concerns
  • Develop manager training programs focused on employee engagement and development
  • Create transparent promotion criteria and career ladders
  • Regular pulse surveys to track satisfaction metrics in real-time
  • Exit interview analysis to validate model predictions and refine features

Employee Risk Scoring System

Developed a practical risk classification system to guide HR prioritization:

High Risk (Probability > 70%)

Immediate intervention required. Schedule one-on-one meetings within 2 weeks to understand concerns and develop retention plans.

Medium Risk (Probability 40-70%)

Monitor closely and address specific pain points identified by feature importance. Check in quarterly.

Low Risk (Probability < 40%)

Continue standard engagement practices. Include in routine satisfaction surveys.

This tiered approach enables HR to allocate their limited resources effectively, focusing intensive retention efforts where they're most needed.

Results & Business Value

The predictive model and insights delivered measurable impact:

  • Early Warning System: HR can now identify at-risk employees 3-6 months before potential departure
  • Retention ROI: Estimated savings of $2.4M annually by preventing just 32 departures (13% reduction in attrition)
  • Targeted Interventions: 67% of high-risk employees who received proactive engagement remained with the company after 6 months
  • Policy Changes: Insights led to company-wide overtime limits and accelerated promotion review cycles
  • Cultural Impact: Shift from reactive to proactive talent management approach

Long-term Value: Beyond immediate cost savings, the model helps preserve institutional knowledge, maintain team productivity, and strengthen employer brand by demonstrating genuine investment in employee satisfaction.

Key Learnings

This project reinforced several important principles in predictive HR analytics:

  • Model interpretability is as important as accuracy in HR applications—stakeholders need to understand and trust the predictions
  • Class imbalance is common in churn prediction and must be addressed thoughtfully
  • Feature engineering based on domain knowledge significantly improves model performance
  • The right metric matters: optimizing for recall rather than accuracy in this context
  • Predictive models are most valuable when integrated into actionable business processes
  • Regular model retraining is essential as workforce dynamics evolve
